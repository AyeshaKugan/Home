Knitting in the Round

There are three different types of needles used when knitting; straight, doubled pointed, and circular. When using straight needles, you can only knit flat, two–dimensional pieces. They are great for beginners, but as you become a more advanced knitter, I would recommend getting double-pointed needles(DPNs) or circular needles. These are used to knit in the round, which allows you to make tubes while you knit. Rounds are used when making socks, hats, and even sweaters. While you can knit these items flat, it requires an extra step of sewing. When knitting flat, you knit one row (the right side), then flip it around and knit the back (the wrong side). Knitting in the round, however you never turn your work and are always knitting on the right side. This makes knitting in the round a lot faster than knitting flat.

Circular Needles vs. Double-Pointed Needles

Aside from how they look, both needles are used to make different items in the round. Double-pointed needles are used to knit smaller items in the round, like socks, gloves, and hats, while circular needles are used for larger items like sweaters. Circular needles are often also used flat to knit larger items such as blankets, as the cord allows it to hold more stitches.

Knitting with Double-Pointed Needles

As the name implies, double-pointed needles have points on both ends allowing you to knit from both sides of it. This helps connect them in the round. Double pointed needles usually come in sets of four or five. The instructions below are written for a set of four, although the same steps apply if you are using five needles.

Step 1: Cast on the number of stitches you need on one needle.

Step 2: Divide the stitches evenly over the three needles (if you're using a set of 5 needles, divide them over 4 needles).

Step 3: Re-arrange them into a triangle, making sure your working yarn is on the right.

Step 4: Insert needle number 4 (the bare one) into the first stitch of needle number 1.

Step 5: Wrap the working yarn from needle number 3 around needles number four, as if to knit.

Step 6: Knit into the first stitch and then pull the stitch off of needle 1. You've now connected in the round.

Step 7: Continue knitting the remaining stitches from needle 1.

Step 8: After you've finished knitting from needle 1, turn the needles so that the working yarn is on the right again.

Repeat steps 4 - 7 for the remaining two needles. Once you've finished knitting the stitches from all three needles, you've completed one row.

Repeat steps 4 - 8 until your piece is as long as you desire. Once you've finished, cast off and weave in the tails on the wrong side of the work.

Knitting with Circular Needles

Circular needles are basically two straight needles except instead of stoppers that have a cord connecting them, allowing you to knit seamless tubes.

Step 1: Cast on the number of stitches you need.

Step 2: A braided edge will be running along the side of your cast-on. Make sure that it is all facing the same direction and not twisted.

Step 3: The working yarn should be on the right needle. Place a stitch marker on the right needle to mark the beginning of each row.

Step 4: Knit into the first stitch to join in the round.

Step 5: Continue knitting all the stitches until you reach the stitch marker.

Step 6: Slip the stitch marker from the left needle to the right and continue on with the next row.
